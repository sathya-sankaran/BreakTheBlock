Chapter Two

Trains of Thought

9 min read 2137 words

TWO

In 1846, the United Kingdom of Great Britain and Ireland passed two hundred and seventy-two Acts of Parliament setting up new railway companies. About a third of the proposed railway routes, totalling fifteen thousand three hundred kilometres, never got built — the companies either collapsed because of poor financial planning, were acquired by larger competitors before they could build their lines, or turned out to be fraudulent enterprises to channel investors' money into other businesses. This was the zenith of a stock market bubble called “Railway Mania”. The price of railway shares increased, speculators invested more money, which further increased the price of railway shares, until the share price collapsed. James Andrew Broun-Ramsay, First Marquess of Dalhousie, was a Scottish leader. As President of the British Board of Trade in 1845, he threw himself into work during the crisis of the Railway Mania with such energy that his health partially broke down under the strain.

He would become important not in his home country, but in another one. He moved to India and served as Governor-General for eight years from 1848. He was popular as Lord Dalhousie and credited with introducing passenger trains on railways, the electric telegraph, and uniform postage in India. He described these as the “three great engines of social improvement”. He also founded the country’s Public Works Department.

Indian Railways Fan Club Academy started as an electronic mailing list in 1989. Its members have documented the history of Indian Railways. It is an excellent source of the organisational and technical history of Indian railways. From their archives, I could piece together the history and the growth of railways in India. It forms a fascinating narrative on how entrepreneurship led to a massive monolith called Indian Railways that, by 2019, was carrying over twenty-three million people and three million metric tonnes of cargo per year.

Trains in India began with British traders wanting to move goods and manage logistics more efficiently. Small experimental railways began popping up in the nineteenth century in Chennai. Back in 1835, they laid the first piece of railway line in Chintadripet in Chennai to see if they could do this at all. They laid the second one in Red Hills, Chennai, a couple of years later to haul stones from a granite quarry. The 1840s saw various proposals from the East and the West of the country. This led to the establishment of the Madras Railway Company and East India Railway Company in 1845. These companies would run their first trains within the next decade, carrying passengers for the first time.

These railway projects spawned lots of proposals from Western India, where the British were much eager to have access between Mumbai (then Bombay) and the rest of India. The then Governor-General, Lord Dalhousie, while advocating for railway construction in India, also stated, “No one can safely say whether or not railways in this country will earn”. Arguably, this led to the Guarantee System.

When the first proposals for railways in India were being debated in Great Britain, there was intense lobbying and support from banks, traders, shipping companies, and other interested parties. These supporters prevailed upon the British Parliament to create the Guarantee System, whereby the government would provide free land and guarantee a certain rate of interest on its capital investment to companies that constructed railways in India. The East India Company honoured this guarantee, which then controlled large parts of India. The railways formed with such agreements governing them were called “Guaranteed Railways”. Typically, the guarantee was for a return of five percent annually, and the right for the railway company to pull out of the venture and get compensation from the Government.

In August 1849, the United Kingdom Parliament passed an act to incorporate the Great Indian Peninsular Railway.

Lord Dalhousie's famous Railway Minute of 1853 laid down the policy that it would allow private enterprises to build railways in India, but that the Government would closely supervise their operations. This was the first time regulation entered the railways.

Several dozen railway companies were incorporated and laying lines by 1870. Private enterprises had built four thousand miles of railway tracks. Governor General Lord Lawrence suggested that the Government of India itself undertake all future construction of railway lines. The idea did not take off. The Government, however, turned away from the guarantee system.

By 1890, the tracks had increased to ten thousand miles, and there were many companies operating with different standards. The Government brought in the first-ever Railway Act, defining the framework for railway construction and operation. The Act acknowledged the various regional railways’ administration and brought in a framework of inspections, safety, and approvals. It proposed to be an inspector of track safety and an ombudsman for resolving interoperability disputes between different local railway administrations.

In 1901, the Government made up a railway board, which formed the core of what we now know as Indian Railways. Four years later, Lord Curzon formalised its powers by attaching it to the Department of Commerce & Industry. Three years later, the Government purchased almost all the lines and re-leased them to private operators. This was the first takeover of private infrastructure building by the Government, but allowed for private players to handle maintenance and operations.

The turning point came in 1920, when, with thirty-seven thousand miles of railway tracks already built, the East India Railway Committee, chaired by Sir William Acworth, recommended a unified management for the railways. On the recommendation of this committee, the Government took over the actual management of all railways and also separated railway finances from the general governmental finances (the latter step led to the practice which lasted till 2017, of presenting the Railway Budget separately from the General Budget every year).

In 1937, the Government created the post of Minister for Transport and Communications. The Minister was a civil servant and could decide on matters dealt with by the Railway Board. Thus began the ministerial oversight of the Railway Board. At Independence in 1947, there were forty-two separate railway systems, including thirty-two lines owned by the former Indian princely states. 1951 marked the formation of zonal railway units beginning with Southern Railway.

In 1989, the Government introduced the new Indian Railways Act, more than a century after the first Act. It kept the words “Railway Administration,” which referred to both a government railway and a railway system built and run by private entrepreneurs. The definition of railways excluded only tramways wholly within a municipal area and lines of rails built in exhibition grounds, fairs, parks, or any other ground solely for recreation. By the time they passed this Act, all railway infrastructure, operations, and maintenance were in the hands of the monolith called Indian Railways. The railways not only ran trains but also set up eleven factories to manufacture coaches, wheels, and everything in between. There was very little private enterprise left in the rail industry.

There were fifty-two thousand kilometres of train lines as of 1950. In the sixty-two years since, they added only eleven thousand kilometres or one-fifth of the capacity to the network. Contrast this with the twenty-one thousand kilometres of highways at the time of independence, which went up six-fold to one hundred and thirty thousand kilometres by 2018.

During the entire history of the Railways, passengers were using trains to travel across the country, but one particular segment had become critical to metropolitan areas — high-frequency connections that ferried office workers and labourers from suburban towns to cities for work.

Suburban trains, also called local trains in India and commuter trains in other parts of the world, are not new. In 1867, the Bombay, Baroda & Central India Railway Company launched the Virar to Bombay Backbay suburban services. It was the first time they introduced a shorter service for passengers. Kolkata opened its suburban railway a year later. In 1928, work began on Madras suburban lines.

The city, by definition, is a trading area. The first cities were essentially marketplaces for people from the hinterlands trading with others within the region and visitors arriving by ships. A city attracts employment from neighbouring towns. Goods and people move into and out of the city. Commuter rails connect towns and satellite areas which are approximately one hour’s distance from the city.

In contrast, Mass Rapid Transit systems, also known as MRTs, serve the metropolitan areas. These are electric traction-led, underground or overhead lines with more frequent stops along the way. In 1890, the City & South London Railway was the first electric-traction rapid transit railway in the world, which was also fully underground. Prior to the opening, the line was named the “City and South London Subway,” thus introducing the term “subway” into railway terminology.

However, it wasn’t until the 1960s that technological advancements made them spread across the world. Since they served within the metropolitan area, they were also called metros in some countries. These MRT systems led to a boom in commuting. The Tokyo Metro system in Japan carries over ten million people every day on its three hundred and twelve-kilometre network with two hundred and forty-nine stations.

Bengaluru had embarked on an ambitious metro project in 2007, the first in South India. The Government had made many failed attempts previously. 1977 was the first time a state town planning team mentioned a metro project. A Southern Railway team in 1981 recommended that the city build a metro system along with commuter rail. It caught the fancy of many people. The late actor Shankar Nag wanted to bring the metro to Bangalore, and the UB group— a company known for its liquor business— tried to build a Light Rail Transit system, a smaller version of the metro. This proposal, however, did not come to fruition.

Finally, the Karnataka Government in 2003 prepared a Detailed Project Report (DPR) for the Bengaluru Metro rail in association with the Delhi Metro Rail Corporation. Delhi had successfully launched its metro system five years earlier.

In fact, the will of the Government to create the metro network was so strong that they created a company called Bangalore Mass Rapid Transit Limited in 1994 and started collecting money from citizens to fund the effort. People were already paying a cess on petrol for thirteen years before the company that would finally build the Metro in Bengaluru— the Bangalore Metro Rail Corporation Limited (BMRCL) — came into existence, superseding the earlier entity which collected the cess. In 2006, the cabinet approved the DPR. In a couple of years, phase one of the project kicked off with an eight-kilometre stretch.

Phase one missed nine deadlines and they revised its budget four times. At the time of approval in 2006, the initial cost estimate for phase one was nearly six thousand four hundred crore rupees. The Government estimated the ultimate cost to build phase one at fourteen thousand four hundred crore rupees. Forty-one percent of this cost came from external borrowings from lending agencies in Japan and banks in India. All these loans meant an interest payment of rupees two crore per day. This was a lot of money to repay and increased the pressure to make the line a success. This would have implications for competing systems down the line.

Steering a ship requires reading the map, deciding, and finding routes that keep the ship headed towards its destination. Rowing requires tactical responses, handling torrid waters, keeping the ship from toppling over, and coordinating the team in the right direction and not at cross purposes.

The job of the government is to steer the ship, not to row it. Delivering services is rowing, and the government is not good at rowing. The Railways Act in 1890 envisaged that the Railway Board would focus on steering, but all that went out of the window decades later.  Indian Railways and BMRCL were both steering and rowing. There were consequences of doing both.

Each stakeholder group had its own unique motivations. The Indian Railways did not want to use its infrastructure to serve suburban commuters because they weren’t making money. BMRCL was eager to serve as many commuters as possible because they had to serve passengers and make money to repay their loans. However, they needed to borrow more to lay new lines. The bus company, BMTC, wasn’t happy to be overshadowed by the new kid on the block. They had already started buying new air-conditioned buses from Volvo to shore up their image.

These dynamics would play out over the duration of a decade-long campaign for commuter rail. BMRCL would fight for its passengers, the BMTC would fight to stay important, and the Indian Railways didn’t care what happened to all the railway lands it was squatting on in the middle of the city. How much of a problem will this turf war be?
